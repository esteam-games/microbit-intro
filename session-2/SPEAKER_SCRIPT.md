## 🎆 SLIDE: How Fireworks Work

"Here's the sequence your code does:

**One** — a single dot appears in the middle. That's your rocket launching.

**Two** — a huge pattern lights up ALL the LEDs. That's the explosion!

**Three** — the screen goes blank. Ready for the next one.

## 💡 SLIDE: Programming Concepts (Fireworks)

"Three big programming ideas in this one:

A **Button Event** — something happens only when you press the button. Your code isn't running all the time — it *waits* until you press it. Like a doorbell!

**Animation Frames** — we show one picture, then a different picture, one after another really fast. That's all animation is!

**Timing** — we use something called a *pause* to control how long each frame shows. Longer pause = slower animation. Shorter pause = faster!"

## 🎬 SLIDE: What is Animation?

---

## 🔍 SLIDE: The Secret of Animation

"Here it is — the big secret.

Animation is just **lots of pictures shown really fast.**

Each picture is called a **frame.** When you show frames fast enough, your brain gets tricked into thinking things are moving!

Movies show **24 frames every single second.** Twenty-four pictures — just in one second!

And guess what? Your micro:bit's 25 LEDs are doing the exact same thing."

---

## 📸 SLIDE: 15 Still Photos

"Look at this. This is one of the oldest animation experiments in history — from way back in **1878!**

A man called Eadweard Muybridge wanted to prove whether a horse's four legs all leave the ground at the same time when it gallops.

*Point at the photos.*

He set up 15 cameras in a row and took 15 photos really fast as a horse ran past.

Each photo is just... a still image. But watch what happens when we put them together and show them really fast...

*Click to next slide.*"

## 🖼️ SLIDE: Be an Animator!

"Here's how you do it.

You design **Frame 1** — your starting picture. Maybe it's a smiley face, a star, anything you want.

Then **Frame 2** — slightly different from Frame 1. Maybe the eyes close a little. Maybe an arm moves.

Then **Frame 3** — change it again.

Then you **loop them together** — 1, 2, 3, 1, 2, 3 — and suddenly your picture is moving!

You're literally making a **mini movie.** From scratch. On a 5×5 screen. How cool is that?"

## 💃 SLIDE: Dance Party!

"Here's what you're building:

A stick figure made of LEDs — it's simpler than it sounds, just dots in the right places for a head, body, arms and legs.

Then you design **different poses.** Arms up, arms down, leaning left, leaning right.

And you switch between poses really fast — and suddenly your stick figure is dancing!

You can even add **music** to match the moves. How cool is that? Real choreography, on a 5×5 screen."

---

## 💡 SLIDE: What you'll learn (Dancing Figure)

"This game teaches you a really important animation tip:

**Small changes between frames = smooth movement.**

If you jump too far between poses — like going from arms up to upside down — it looks jumpy and weird.

But if you move just a little bit each frame — the brain sees it as smooth motion.

That's exactly what professional animators at Disney and Pixar do! They move characters tiny amounts, frame by frame, to make things look natural."

---

## 🧱 SLIDE: MakeCode Blocks (Dancing Figure)

"Look at the blocks. We have **three functions** — pose1, pose2, pose3. Each function is just a named chunk of code.

*Point at the functions.*

Pose 1 = arms up. Pose 2 = arms out. Pose 3 = leaning.

Then when Button A is pressed, we call pose1.
Button B = pose2.
Shake = a loop that cycles through all the poses really fast — that's the random dance!

See how we split the code into named chunks? That's called **functions** — and it's something real programmers use every single day."

## 💡 General Tips

- **Mirror their energy** — if they're excited about something, match it
- **Ask lots of questions** — "What do you think happens if...?" keeps them engaged
- **Celebrate loudly** when something works, even small things
- **Walk the room** during coding time — one-on-one encouragement goes a long way
- **Pace yourself** — it's fine to skip a sub-slide if you're running short on time
- The **interactive horse animation** on the "You Control the Speed!" slide always gets a great reaction — linger here
